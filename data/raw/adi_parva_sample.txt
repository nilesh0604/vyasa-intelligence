THE MAHABHARATA

ADI PARVA (The Book of the Beginning)

SECTION I

Om! Having bowed down to Narayana, and Nara, the foremost of men, as also to the goddess Saraswati, must the word Jaya be uttered.

Ugrasrava, the son of Lomaharshana, surnamed Sauti, well-versed in the Puranas, bending with humility, one day approached the great sages of rigid vows, sitting in their hermitage dedicated to the sacrifice of the Brahmanas, who were attended by the celebrated disciple of Vyasa, and who had all performed the sacrifice extending over twelve years. And he saluted those Munis with his head bent low, and said, "I bow to you, O Brahmanas, may you be pleased with me. I am Sauti, the son of Lomaharshana, and I am here to listen to the sacred stories."

The sages said, "Welcome, O Sauti! We are pleased with you. Tell us what you desire to hear."

Sauti replied, "I desire to hear the sacred Mahabharata, the story of the great battle between the Pandavas and the Kauravas, composed by the great sage Vyasa."

SECTION II

Vyasa, the son of Satyavati, having composed the Mahabharata, taught it to his disciple Vaisampayana. Vaisampayana, having learned it from his preceptor, recited it in the presence of Janamejaya, the son of Parikshit, at the serpent-sacrifice of that king.

Janamejaya, the son of Parikshit, the Kuru king, having performed the sacrifice of snakes, was desirous of hearing the history of his ancestors. And he asked Vaisampayana, "O Brahmana, recite to me the history of my ancestors, the kings of the Kuru race."

Vaisampayana said, "I will recite to you the history of your ancestors, as I have heard it from my preceptor Vyasa."

SECTION III

In the ancient city of Hastinapura, there lived a king named Shantanu, the descendant of the Bharata race. He was virtuous and mighty, and ruled his kingdom with justice and wisdom.

One day, while walking along the banks of the Ganga, the king saw a beautiful maiden of divine appearance. She was none other than the goddess Ganga, who had taken human form. Shantanu fell in love with her and asked her to be his wife.

Ganga agreed to marry him on one condition: that he would never question her actions, whatever they might be. Shantanu accepted the condition, and they were married.

SECTION IV

In time, Ganga gave birth to a son. But as soon as the child was born, she took it to the river and drowned it. Shantanu, bound by his promise, could not say anything. This happened seven times, and seven sons were lost to the river.

When the eighth son was born, Shantanu could not bear it any longer. He stopped Ganga from taking the child to the river and asked her why she had killed their seven sons.

Ganga revealed that she was the goddess Ganga and that the eight children were the eight Vasus, who had been cursed to be born as mortals. She had been freeing them from their curse by drowning them as soon as they were born. The eighth Vasu, however, was destined to live a long life on earth.

Ganga then left Shantanu, taking the eighth son with her. She promised to return him to Shantanu when he was old enough to be his father's heir.

SECTION V

Years passed, and Shantanu ruled his kingdom alone. One day, while hunting in the forest, he came across a young boy who was playing with a bow and arrows. The boy was none other than his son, whom Ganga had named Devavrata.

Shantanu was overjoyed to see his son and brought him back to Hastinapura. Devavrata grew up to be a mighty warrior, skilled in all arms and learned in all scriptures.

When Shantanu fell in love with Satyavati, the daughter of a fisherman, her father agreed to the marriage only on the condition that her son would inherit the kingdom. To fulfill his father's desire, Devavrata took a terrible oath: he would never marry and would remain celibate all his life.

For this great sacrifice, he was called Bhishma, the terrible one. Bhishma became the guardian of the Kuru dynasty and served his father and his half-brothers with unwavering devotion.

SECTION VI

Satyavati bore two sons to Shantanu: Chitrangada and Vichitravirya. Chitrangada died young, fighting a Gandharva. Vichitravirya, though young, was installed on the throne.

Bhishma, acting as regent, went to the kingdom of Kashi to find suitable brides for Vichitravirya. In the swayamvara of the three princesses of Kashi—Amba, Ambika, and Ambalika—Bhishma won them all and brought them to Hastinapura.

But Amba had already given her heart to the king of Salva. When Bhishma learned of this, he let her go. However, the king of Salva rejected her, and Amba, filled with rage against Bhishma, sought revenge. She performed severe austerities and obtained a boon from Lord Shiva: she would be the cause of Bhishma's death.

In her next life, she was born as Shikhandi, the daughter of Drupada, and later became a man. It was Shikhandi who, in the great battle of Kurukshetra, caused Bhishma's death.

SECTION VII

Vichitravirya died without leaving any heirs. Satyavati, concerned about the continuation of the Kuru line, called upon her son Vyasa, who was born before her marriage to Shantanu.

Vyasa, through his ascetic powers, granted sons to Vichitravirya's widows. Ambika gave birth to Dhritarashtra, who was born blind, and Ambalika gave birth to Pandu, who was born pale.

When Vyasa was asked to grant another son to Ambika, she sent her maid instead. The maid gave birth to Vidura, who was wise and virtuous, though born of a maid.

Dhritarashtra married Gandhari, who blindfolded herself for life to share her husband's darkness. Pandu married Kunti and Madri. Gandhari gave birth to a hundred sons, the Kauravas, with Duryodhana as the eldest. Kunti gave birth to three sons: Yudhishthira, Bhima, and Arjuna. Madri gave birth to two sons: Nakula and Sahadeva. These five brothers were known as the Pandavas.

SECTION VIII

The Pandavas and Kauravas grew up together, but there was always rivalry between them. Duryodhana, jealous of the Pandavas' prowess and popularity, plotted against them.

The Kuru prince Duryodhana, along with his brothers and his uncle Shakuni, built a house of lac for the Pandavas in Varanavata. The house was designed to be set on fire, killing the Pandavas inside.

But Vidura, the wise uncle, warned the Pandavas of the plot. They escaped through a tunnel and lived in disguise in the forest.

SECTION IX

During their exile, the Pandavas heard about the swayamvara of Draupadi, the daughter of King Drupada of Panchala. Arjuna, disguised as a Brahmana, won the swayamvara by piercing the eye of a fish rotating on a wheel, looking only at its reflection in the water.

The Pandavas returned home with Draupadi. Kunti, without seeing what they had brought, told them to share whatever they had obtained. Thus, Draupadi became the wife of all five Pandavas.

SECTION X

King Dhritarashtra, learning that the Pandavas were alive, gave them half the kingdom. The Pandavas established their capital at Indraprastha and ruled their kingdom wisely.

Yudhishthira performed the Rajasuya sacrifice, establishing his supremacy over all kings. Duryodhana, jealous of the Pandavas' prosperity, challenged Yudhishthira to a game of dice.

In the game, Yudhishthira lost everything: his kingdom, his brothers, and even Draupadi. Draupadi was dragged into the assembly and insulted by Duryodhana and his brother Dushasana.

It was at this moment that Draupadi prayed to Lord Krishna, who miraculously provided her with an endless stream of cloth when Dushasana tried to disrobe her.

Dhritarashtra, fearing the consequences, restored the Pandavas' kingdom and sent them into exile for twelve years, with an additional year of living in disguise.
